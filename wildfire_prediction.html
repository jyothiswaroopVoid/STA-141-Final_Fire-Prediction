Predicting Notable Wildfire Incidence in the USA

STA 141A Final Project: Jyothiswaroop Makala

0.1 Abstract

This report develops a predictive model for the monthly incidence of notable wildfires across U.S. counties from 1992 to 2015. Using a county-month panel dataset constructed from historical wildfire records, land cover data, and monthly climate measurements, we define a binary outcome indicating whether at least one wildfire of 300 or more acres occurred in a given county and month. We fit two models: a logistic regression baseline and a random forest. Models are evaluated using time-aware expanding-window cross-validation to prevent data leakage, and performance is assessed via the Area Under the ROC Curve (AUC). Our exploratory analysis reveals strong seasonality, pronounced geographic clustering in the western United States, and clear associations between fire incidence and drought, temperature, and land cover type. The random forest modestly outperforms logistic regression, with both models achieving meaningful predictive signal above chance.

1 Data Preprocessing

1.1 Overview

The modeling dataset is constructed by joining three sources:

  • wildfires: individual fire records with location (state, county FIPS), discovery date, and burned area
  • county_climate: monthly county-level temperature, precipitation, and drought index (PDSI) from 1992–2015
  • county_land_cover: annual county-level land cover fractions from the NLCD

The prediction task is a binary classification at the county × month level: did at least one notable wildfire occur?

1.2 Notable Fire Cutoff

Following operational wildfire reporting standards used by U.S. fire management agencies, we define a notable fire as any fire with a final burned area of ≥ 300 acres. This threshold corresponds to the standard for large wildland fires in grass or brush fuel models, and filters out the vast majority of small, quickly-contained fires that would otherwise create extreme class imbalance and obscure meaningful fire dynamics.

1.3 Data Extraction

  • wildfires Wildfire records were sourced from the 1.88 Million US Wildfires dataset on Kaggle, derived from the Fire Program Analysis Fire-Occurrence Database (FPA-FOD), and loaded directly into R from the SQLite database FPA_FOD_20170508.sqlite.

  • county_climate I obtained climate data from the NOAA nClimDiv database using an R script that programmatically downloaded and parsed four county-level variables — tmpc, tmax, tmin, and pcpn — directly from the NOAA server. Since PDSI is only available at the climate division level, I joined it to the county data using NOAA’s official county-to-climdivs.txt crosswalk, mapping NOAA’s internal state codes to standard 5-digit Census FIPS for consistent joining. The final output county_climate_1990_2016.csv contains one row per county-month filtered to 1992–2015.

  • county_land_cover I got land cover data from the National Land Cover Database (NLCD) using Google Earth Engine (GEE). I wrote a Python script to extract annual county-level land cover fractions from 1992 to 2015 by retrieving each year’s image from the USGS/ANNUAL_NLCD/LANDCOVER collection and computing, for each of the 3,108 U.S. counties, the fraction of area belonging to each of 16 land cover classes — including water, developed land, forest types, shrubland, cropland, and wetlands. I created a binary indicator band per class, summed pixel counts within county boundaries using TIGER/2018/Counties at 300-meter resolution (EPSG:5070), and divided by total valid pixels to obtain fractional coverage. Results were exported annually to Google Drive, then merged into a single panel nlcd_county_1992_2015.csv containing one row per county-year with 16 frac_ variables, which I joined to the climate and wildfire panel by county_fips and year.

Note: All the datasets are available in the GitHub repository linked in this report.

1.5 Filtering and Aggregating Wildfires

The raw wildfire records are filtered to notable fires (≥ 300 acres), calendar dates are reconstructed from FIRE_YEAR and DISCOVERY_DOY, and county identifiers are standardized to 5-digit Census FIPS codes. The filtered records are then collapsed to a county-month panel where each row represents a unique county-month in which at least one notable fire was recorded.

Show code 
# Step 1: Filter notable fires (>= 300 acres) and extract year/month
fires_notable <- wildfires %>%
  filter(FIRE_SIZE >= 300) %>%
  mutate(
    # Use FIRE_YEAR + DISCOVERY_DOY to build calendar date
    discovery_date2 = as.Date(paste0(FIRE_YEAR, "-01-01")) +
                      days(DISCOVERY_DOY - 1),
    year      = FIRE_YEAR,
    month_num = month(discovery_date2),
    # Standardize FIPS: STATE (2-digit) + COUNTY (3-digit) -> 5-char string
    county_fips = str_pad(as.character(as.integer(
                    paste0(str_pad(as.character(as.integer(
                      match(STATE, state.abb))), 2, pad = "0"),
                      str_pad(FIPS_CODE, 3, pad = "0")))),
                    5, pad = "0")
  ) %>%
  filter(!is.na(county_fips), !is.na(year), !is.na(month_num))

# Step 2: Collapse to county-month binary outcome
# y = 1 if at least one notable fire occurred
fire_panel <- fires_notable %>%
  group_by(county_fips, year, month_num) %>%
  summarise(
    fire_count    = n(),
    max_fire_size = max(FIRE_SIZE),
    .groups = "drop"
  ) %>%
  mutate(
    fire_occurred = 1L,
    # Ensure county_fips is character zero-padded (matches climate spine)
    county_fips = str_pad(county_fips, width = 5, pad = "0")
  )

1.6 Building the Full County-Month Panel

A panel is created so that — every county-month combination is represented, with fire_occurred = 0 assigned to any county-month with no recorded notable fire. Land Cover fractions are matched to each year using the nearest available NLCD survey year. The County Centroids are computed and joined to the panel, adding latitude and longitude as geographic predictors.At the end we add Fire Rolling History that computes lagged 3, 6, and 12-month rolling averages of past fire activity for each county, capturing short, medium, and long-term fire history as additional predictors.

# Step 3: Build the complete county-month grid from climate data
# (climate already covers all counties x months; use as the spine)
panel_base <- county_climate %>%
  select(county_fips, state_name, year, month_num, 
         tmpc, tmax, tmin, pcpn, pdsi) %>%
  filter(year >= 1992, year <= 2015)

# Step 4: Join wildfire outcome (left join → unobserved = no fire)
panel <- panel_base %>%
  left_join(fire_panel, by = c("county_fips", "year", "month_num")) %>%
  mutate(
    fire_occurred = replace_na(fire_occurred, 0L),
    fire_count    = replace_na(fire_count, 0L)
  )

# Step 5: Join land cover (annual; join on county_fips + year)
# NLCD updates infrequently — forward-fill to all years

                               width = 5, pad = "0"))

  left_join(
    land_cover_clean %>% rename(lc_year = year),
    by = c("county_fips", "lc_year")

Creates Rolling Fire Rate

Show code 
# Step 7: Rolling fire rate per county at 3, 6, and 12-month windows (lagged — no leakage)
# Captures short-, medium-, and long-term fire history in each county
panel <- panel %>%
  arrange(county_fips, year, month_num) %>%
  group_by(county_fips) %>%
  mutate(
    fire_roll3 = slide_dbl(
      fire_occurred,
      mean,
      .before = 3, .after = -1,   # lag: excludes current month, 3-month window
      .complete = FALSE
    ),
    fire_roll6 = slide_dbl(
      fire_occurred,
      mean,
      .before = 6, .after = -1,   # lag: excludes current month, 6-month window
      .complete = FALSE
    ),
    fire_roll12 = slide_dbl(
      fire_occurred,
      mean,
      .before = 12, .after = -1,  # lag: excludes current month, 12-month window
      .complete = FALSE
    )
  ) %>%
  ungroup()

1.7 Handling Missing Data

Missing values are addressed through two targeted imputation strategies: land cover fractions with no NLCD record are filled with 0, and missing climate variables are replaced with the county’s past-years median for that specific month to avoid data leakage. Any rows still missing key predictors after imputation are dropped, ensuring a complete dataset for modeling.

Show code 
# Replace NA land cover fractions with 0 (county had no NLCD record)
lc_cols <- names(panel)[str_starts(names(panel), "frac_")]
panel <- panel %>%
  mutate(across(all_of(lc_cols), ~ replace_na(.x, 0)))

# Replace NA climate with county-month median of past years only (no leakage)
panel <- panel %>%
  arrange(county_fips, month_num, year) %>%
  group_by(county_fips, month_num) %>%
  mutate(across(c(tmpc, tmax, tmin, pcpn, pdsi),
                ~ ifelse(is.na(.x),
                         slide_dbl(.x, ~ median(.x, na.rm = TRUE),
                                   .before = Inf, .after = -1,
                                   .complete = FALSE),
                         .x))) %>%
  ungroup()

# Drop rows still missing after imputation (very few)
panel <- panel %>% drop_na(tmpc, pcpn, pdsi, fire_roll3, fire_roll6, fire_roll12, centroid_lat, centroid_lon)

3 Modeling

3.1 Predictor and Response Specification

The response variable is y_next: a binary indicator equal to 1 if at least one notable wildfire (≥ 300 acres) occurred in a given county during the following month. Using the next month’s outcome while conditioning on the current month’s predictors creates a genuine forecasting setup and prevents data leakage.

Removed land-cover predictors that accounted for less than 1% correlation, and removed precipitation predictor because the density distribution between the fire and no-fires is identical to prevent the dataset from being too noisy for the model to accurately predict fires.

pdsi Climate Drought index — integrates moisture deficit over time
centroid_lat, centroid_lon Geographic Geographic coordinates capture regional fire regimes and the West-East ecological gradient
frac_forest_eve Geographic Evergreen forest — high-density fuel load, concentrated in fire-prone western landscapes
frac_forest_mix Geographic Mixed forest — transitional fuel type between evergreen and deciduous cover

3.2 Baseline Model: Logistic Regression

Logistic regression models the log-odds of a notable wildfire occurring as a linear combination of predictors:

\[ \log \frac{P(Y=1 \mid \mathbf{x})}{1 - P(Y=1 \mid \mathbf{x})} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \texttt{month\_sin} + \beta_2 \cdot \texttt{month\_cos} + \cdots + \beta_{p-2} \cdot \texttt{fire\_roll3} + \beta_{p-1} \cdot \texttt{fire\_roll6} + \beta_p \cdot \texttt{fire\_roll12} \]

This model is interpretable (each \(\hat{\beta}_j\) gives a log-odds change per unit increase in predictor \(j\)), fast to fit, and serves as a strong, transparent benchmark. It assumes predictors act additively on the log-odds scale — a useful simplification that may underfit complex nonlinear interactions (e.g., combined drought + heat + fuel load).

3.3 Second Model: Random Forest

The random forest relaxes the linearity assumption by fitting an ensemble of decision trees on bootstrapped subsets of the data and averaging their predictions. Each tree splits on a random subset of predictors at each node, reducing correlation among trees and improving generalization. Formally, the predicted probability is:

\[ \hat{y} = \frac{1}{B} \sum_{b=1}^{B} T_b(\mathbf{x}) \]

where \(T_b\) is the \(b\)-th decision tree trained on a bootstrap sample. We use the randomForest package with:

  • Number of trees: 500
  • Variables tried per split (mtry): default (\(\lfloor\sqrt{p}\rfloor\))
  • Minimum node size: 10

The correlation heatmap above summarizes the predictors used in both models. The three temperature variables (tmpc, tmax, tmin) share correlations of .97–.99, creating multicollinearity that destabilizes logistic regression coefficients — random forest handles this naturally through feature subsampling at each split. The strong correlation between centroid_lon and frac_shrub (r = -.51) reflects a West-East ecological gradient where fire risk transitions sharply near the 100th meridian, a geographic threshold that a linear decision boundary cannot capture. The lagged fire history variables (fire_roll3, fire_roll6, fire_roll12) encode path dependency — counties that have burned repeatedly are structurally more fire-prone, and random forest can condition on these rolling histories jointly with climate and land cover. Together, these structural features of the data make random forest the natural second model.

4 Model Fitting and Validation

4.1 Time-Aware Expanding-Window Cross-Validation

To respect the temporal structure of the data and avoid data leakage, we use an expanding-window validation scheme:

  • For each fold with training cutoff year \(t\): train on all years \(\leq t\), validate on year \(t+1\)
  • Minimum training window: 5 years (to ensure sufficient data before first evaluation)
  • This mirrors real-world deployment: the model only ever sees past data

downsample_train <- function(df, ratio = 5) {
  fire    <- df %>% filter(y_next == 1)
  no_fire <- df %>% filter(y_next == 0) %>% slice_sample(n = min(nrow(.), nrow(fire) * ratio))
  bind_rows(fire, no_fire)
}

5 Summary

5.1 Key EDA Findings

Exploratory analysis revealed three dominant patterns in U.S. wildfire incidence:

  1. Geographic concentration: Notable fires are heavily concentrated in counties with dense conifer and evergreen forest cover that experience prolonged dry seasons — particularly California, the Pacific Northwest, and the Rocky Mountain states. These counties share a critical combination: high fuel loads from continuous forest canopy and chronic moisture deficits that dry that fuel to ignition thresholds by mid-summer. A secondary cluster appears across the Southeast, especially Florida and Georgia, where pine flatwoods and frequent spring drought create a distinct but less intense fire regime.

  2. Strong seasonality: Fire incidence peaks sharply in summer (June–July), driven by the seasonal drying cycle in heavily forested western counties where snowmelt transitions rapidly into months of near-zero precipitation. Annual fire rates have trended upward since the early 2000s, with the most extreme years (2006 and 2012) coinciding with documented nationwide drought events that pushed already-dry forested counties past critical fuel moisture thresholds.

  3. Climate signal: Fire county-months show substantially higher temperatures and lower PDSI than no-fire county-months, while precipitation distributions overlap considerably between the two groups, suggesting that accumulated moisture deficit over prior months matters far more than any single month’s rainfall. This is consistent with the forested fire regime: forests accumulate fuel year-round, and it is the depth and duration of seasonal drying that determines whether that fuel ignites at scale.

5.2 Model Performance

Both models achieve meaningful predictive performance above chance across all validation years. The random forest outperforms logistic regression with a mean cross-validated AUC of 0.9025 (SD = 0.03) compared to 0.8211 (SD = 0.05) for logistic regression — a gap of roughly 8 AUC points that reflects the random forest’s ability to exploit correlated predictors, geographic thresholds, and lagged fire history jointly. The random forest also shows greater consistency across validation folds, with a higher minimum AUC of 0.7948 versus 0.6796 for logistic regression, suggesting it degrades less severely in atypical fire years.

The logistic regression baseline remains competitive and offers a key practical advantage: coefficient interpretability. Its performance show that the chosen predictors carry genuine predictive signal and that the modeling setup is sound.

5.3 Limitations and Future Directions

The current model operates at the county-month level, which aggregates considerable spatial heterogeneity within large western counties, a particular limitation given that fire risk within a single large forested county can vary dramatically by elevation, aspect, and distance from roads. Finer spatial resolution (e.g., grid cells) or inclusion of topographic variables (slope, aspect, elevation) could substantially improve performance in these high-risk forested regions. Additionally, ignition sources (lightning vs. human) are not included as predictors; human ignition risk covaries with proximity to roads and population density, which future work could incorporate. Vapor Pressure Deficit (VPD), considered by modern fire science as a strong meteorological predictor of fire weather in forested landscapes, is also absent from the current feature set and represents the highest-value addition for future modeling efforts.
